# Augustine's Adoption of Stoic Determinism: A Critique from Scripture

## Systematic Theology Notes

---

## I. Introduction: Augustine and the Stoic Doctrine of Divine Causation

Augustine adopted a doctrine from the Stoics that he believed to be entirely true, and this doctrine has profoundly colored much of Reformed theology. The Stoic teaching held that **nothing could take place unless God was actively, directly causing it**. For example, the Stoics believed that if a leaf fell from a tree, it was God Himself making that leaf fall. Augustine incorporated this concept wholesale into his Christian theology.

I disagree with this position. I do not see it as biblical.

I do believe in the sovereignty of God. I do believe in predestination. But I believe the scriptural picture is different from what Augustine presented. Where Augustine taught that God's finger is directly pulling the leaf from the tree, I believe that **God set all of creation up with laws, and everything operates according to those laws.** While God's life permeates the universe, it is not His direct, physical doing in every instance. He established laws so that creation did not need His continual intervention. It operates—and the critical point is that it operates—without His continual intervention.

> *For a full treatment of this position, see my essay "The Demarcation Line."*

---

## II. The Problem of Evil Under Augustine's Framework

If you follow Augustine's idea to its logical conclusion, **you must ascribe evil to God.** If God is directly causing every sprouting blade of grass from its seed, then He is also directly causing every act of violence and wickedness. This is untenable.

But if you hold that God set laws in motion and humanity came up underneath those laws through the Fall, the problem of evil finds a different and more scriptural resolution.

There are two critical objections to Augustine's position:

### A. It Contradicts God's Rest

The Bible says that **God rested from all His labors** (Genesis 2:2). He set everything in motion, and it now works according to His perfect plan. He does not need to be continually working within it at every granular level. Augustine's Stoic-derived theology places God perpetually laboring inside creation, but Scripture says He rested—because His work of creation was complete and perfect.

### B. It Requires God to Be the Author of Evil

Under this framework, if someone commits murder, that was God in there doing it—or at the very least, it was God's will that it should happen. This is precisely where Reformed theologians run afoul. **You cannot ascribe evil to God.** Scripture is clear on this point.

---

## III. Where the Reformed Theologians Got It Right—and Wrong

### What They Got Right: God's Omniscience and Sovereignty

The Reformed tradition is correct that God knows all things. He is omniscient and sovereign. You do not cross over into the Arminian doctrine that human free choice can supersede God's power. God's knowledge encompasses all things.

### What They Got Wrong: Conflating Knowledge with Causation

There is a **nuance** between the Reformed and Arminian positions that I believe the Reformed theologians have missed by adopting Augustine's Stoic framework. Augustine took a non-biblical idea—that God is directly doing all of this—and it runs afoul of the teachings of Christ Himself.

**Notice Christ's own wording in Matthew 10:29:**

> *"Are not two sparrows sold for a farthing? and one of them shall not fall on the ground without your Father."*

Christ said a sparrow cannot fall to the ground **but that God is aware of it**—not that God caused it to fall. Under Augustine's theology, you would have to reword Christ's statement: "A sparrow cannot fall to the ground but that God did it." But that is not what Christ said. Christ said God *knows about it.*

The concept is this: **Not every hardship is because of God, but there cannot be a hardship without God being aware of it.**

Christ goes on to say that your heavenly Father knows everything you have need of, and that God will supply your needs and work in your life (Matthew 6:32–33). God is aware of the plight of mankind and will intervene to help—but it is not that God is *causing* the plight.

---

## IV. The Current State of Creation Is Not God's Design

Augustine and the Reformed theologians envision that nothing is out of kilter in the economy of God. But that vision belongs to the **new heavens and the new earth.** The reason things are out of kilter *now* is because **humanity placed themselves beneath creation** through the Fall.

### The Reformed Formulation: *Posse Peccare* and *Posse Non Peccare*

This is one area where Augustine and the Reformed tradition got it right:

- **Before the Fall:** *Posse peccare et posse non peccare*—the ability to sin and the ability not to sin.
- **After the Fall:** *Non posse non peccare*—the inability not to sin.

Humanity was created with the capacity to fall and the capacity not to fall. But when Adam sinned, through **federal headship**, he threw all of humanity underneath the created order.

## V. Creation as a Self-Operating Machine: The Distinction Between Nature and Moral Beings

God created everything to bring forth after its kind—a good, working machine. But humanity was not made in the same respect as the rest of creation, because **humanity was given will.**

### The Nature of Non-Moral Creation

Consider an engine: it simply works the way the engineer designed it. An internal combustion engine cannot decide one day, "I don't want to be an engine anymore; I want to be a grill and cook meat." It must do what it was designed to do.

All of God's natural creation operates this way:
- A dog must be a dog.
- A squirrel cannot decide to become a philosopher.
- The sun cannot decide it no longer wants to light the solar system and would rather become a gas giant like Jupiter.

This is what God declared "good"—a creation that did not need His continual intervention. It runs according to the laws He established.

### The Nature of Moral Beings

However, humanity was not designed this way. Mankind was created **a little higher**—in the same class as angels, cherubim, and seraphim. Like God Himself, humanity was given **free choice.** Humanity was created *above* the natural order, not within it.

> *See "The Demarcation Line" for the full theological framework of this distinction.*

## VI. The Command and the Fall: Don't Place Yourself Beneath Creation

God gave one explicit command: **do not put yourself beneath the natural order.** If you place yourself beneath it, there will be a catastrophic problem—because the natural world runs without God's continual intervention. It runs without internal decision-making power. It simply runs.

If you place yourself beneath it, **it will devour you without mercy,** because it operates without compassion and without choice.

### The Apprentice Analogy

Consider an apprentice in a metalworking shop. The master tells him: "You can be artistic, use these tools, and create—but do not put your hand in that machine. If you do, it will not have mercy on you. It will devour your hand." The machine runs without compassion and without decision-making. God created all of nature to run in exactly this way—like a well-oiled machine, without decision-making capabilities.

This was the substance of God's command to humanity: **do not place yourself beneath the created order, because if you do, it will consume you.**

## VII. The Machine Shop Analogy: Federal Headship, the Fall, and Redemption

### The Shop Running Well

Picture an old-fashioned steam-powered machine shop—belts running, machines humming, everything operating meticulously. You fire it up in the morning and it runs. But it requires a **caretaker**: bearings must be oiled, belts must be maintained, and someone must oversee the operation.

Humanity was appointed as this caretaker. Everything was under his stewardship.

### The Fall: The Caretaker Fails

The engineer tells the shop steward: "This is your shop. Maintain it. But don't get careless—if you do, you'll fall into one of those belts and it will devour you."

But the steward doesn't listen. He falls asleep on the job. He gets tangled in the belts and is torn up. Now look at the shop: **bearings are squeaking, belts are flying off, smoke is rising from unlubricated joints.** Chaos everywhere.

To say that the engineer who designed the shop *desired* this outcome is ludicrous. The shop is in this condition because **the person whose job it was to oversee it did not listen to the engineer.** He got himself tangled beneath the machinery.

### This Is What Adam Did

Adam threw himself beneath the created order. Because humanity—under Adam's federal headship—is now beneath creation rather than over it, **all creation is out of order.** This is why we see earthquakes, floods, disease, and death. This is why Paul wrote:

> *"For the whole creation groans and travails in pain together until now."* (Romans 8:22)

Creation is not running according to the way the Engineer designed it. **God did not design this world to run this way.** The caretaker is incapacitated, and we, being under Adam's federal headship, share in the consequence.

### God's Awareness and Present Help

It is not the will of God that the shop is in disarray. But consider: **the Engineer has not abandoned the shop.** He rested from building it—His construction work is complete—but He knows exactly what is happening.

He hears the belts squealing. He sees the bearings smoking. He is fully aware. And He can come over to your individual workstation and help you, even while the whole shop is not yet running optimally.

This is what Christ meant: a sparrow cannot fall to the ground without the great Creator knowing about it. He is completely aware. He knows what is going on. He knows about you, too. **Raise your hand and ask Him to come over—the Engineer is in the shop.**

### Redemption: The Engineer Becomes the Shop Steward

What must happen to restore the shop?

1. **First**, the Engineer must extract humanity from being tangled in the machinery—and this is what He is doing through **Calvary.**
2. **Then**, He must repair the shop—and Scripture tells us He will make **a new heavens and a new earth** (Revelation 21:1).
3. **Finally**, to ensure it never happens again, **He will make Himself the Shop Steward.** Through Christ, everyone comes under a new federal headship. Under Christ's stewardship, creation cannot get out of kilter again.

---

## VIII. Conclusion

Augustine got some things right, but his adoption of Stoic determinism—the idea that God is directly causing every event in creation—is not biblical. It contradicts God's rest, it ascribes evil to God, and it misreads Christ's own words about the Father's knowledge versus the Father's causation.

The scriptural picture is this:
- **God created a self-sustaining order governed by laws.**
- **Humanity was placed above that order as its steward, endowed with free will.**
- **Through the Fall, humanity placed itself beneath that order, and creation fell into disorder.**
- **God is fully aware of all that happens and intervenes to help those who call on Him.**
- **Through Christ and Calvary, God is rescuing humanity from beneath the machinery of the fallen order.**
- **In the new heavens and new earth, Christ Himself will serve as the eternal Steward, and creation will never fall out of order again.**

The distinction is not between sovereignty and no sovereignty. It is between **causation and knowledge**—between God doing every single thing and God knowing every single thing. Scripture teaches the latter, not the former.
